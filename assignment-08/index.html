How To Recover a Heart

Brought to you by:

A Trained Tissue Recovery Specialist

This is an overview of a recovery procedure once a sterile field has been established, the recovery site is prepped and draped, and operator is at the point of commencing recovery.

Illustrations:

Instructions:
  1. Perform sterile prep and drape off the recovery zone.

  2. The are many ways to make the chest incision--this tutorial demonstrates a "U" incision.
  1. Make your "U" incision and remove the chest plate.

    (Utilize the contact page if you need more details on removing the chest plate)

  2. Take two lap sponges and tuck them around the lung tissue to isolate it from the pericardial sack (lungs are crawling with bacteria because they have a direct interface with the outside air)

  3. Use forceps to clamp off the Inferior Vena Cava (we don't want fluid to flood the thoracic cavity)

  4. Incise the pericardium to reveal the heart and head vessels.
  1. Use towel clamps to pull back and affix the four corners of the pericardium to the sides of the thoracic cavity.

  2. The goal is to maximize the lengths of the head and pulmonary vessels.

    The Three Head vessels are the:
    • Subclavian Artery
    • Brachial Artery
    • Carotid Arterty

    The Pulmonaries carry blood to and from the lungs. Each lung is fed by one artery and one vein (the only place in the body where veins carry oxygenated blood and the arteries carry the deoxygenated blood.)

    Be sure to follow these vessels 1 cm beyond the point where they bifurcate and dive through the pericardial sack into the lungs:
    • Left and Right Pulmonary Arteries
    • Left and Right Pulmonary Veins

  3. There are more attachments around back, but once you've carefully recovered your vessel, the rest can be incised until the heart is free.

Congratulations! You've recovered a heart for valvular transplant ! Now you can package it and ship it away to be cleaned, processed, and packaged into a surgical graft.
